
It's Tuesday, October 24th, 1978

The air itself feels heavy and old, it's a distinctly urban autumn smell, a dense mixture of damp, decaying leaves on the pavement, the sharp, mineral scent of recent rain, and the ever-present, biting undertone of low-grade coal smoke hanging in the cool air. The breeze isn't strong, but it's a persistent, chilly draft that finds its way through the collar of your coat, carrying with it the faint, grimy smell of diesel from a passing truck. The temperature hovers in that uncomfortable zone where it's too cold to stand still but not yet cold enough for the satisfying deep freeze of winter.

Visually, the world is a study in muted tones. The sky is a uniform, washed-out slate gray, a lid on the city that makes the brutalist architecture feel even more imposing. The sunlight is weak and indirect, draining the color from the yellow leaves and casting long, soft-edged shadows. The streets are damp, reflecting the gray light with a slick, oily sheen.

The sounds are just as subdued. There is the distant, rhythmic rumble of the U-Bahn running beneath the streets, a vibration you feel in the soles of your shoes. The traffic is sparse by modern standards, the chugging, two-stroke engines of small, state-approved cars and the heavier groan of city buses. What's most noticeable is the lack of casual noise. There isn't much laughter or loud conversation. People walk with a brisk, purposeful stride, their faces set in a neutral, unreadable expression. It's the quiet hum of a populace that has learned not to draw attention to itself.

Approximately at 10:15 AM, a Staatspost worker approaches the Sonnenblick complex at Schillerstraße 15. He moves with a detached, bureaucratic efficiency, his appearance a clear statement of his state function. He wears a somber, hip-length jacket of Feldgrau wool, its simple, modern cut practical and severe. Beneath it, a thin black tie is knotted neatly over a plain, light gray work shirt.

As he nears the entrance, the building's massive form creates a sudden stillness, the air calm and cool. The details of his uniform become sharper: the small metal cockade of the Staatspost on his peaked service cap, the jarring splash of bright yellow piping on his collar, and the VDR eagle emblem stamped onto the polished metal buttons of his jacket.

The building's massive form created a sudden stillness at the entrance, the air calm and cool. Inside Tower B, the heavy glass doors closed, muffling the street sounds and replacing them with the soft, tinny notes of lobby music. The air was heated and still, carrying the layered scents of floor polish from the morning cleaning, old paper from countless letters, and the faint, domestic smell of cooking from the apartments above. Under the steady hum of the fluorescent lights, the lobby's acoustics carried every small sound with surprising clarity, the metallic click of a mailbox lock, the soft squeak of rubber soles on linoleum, a distant cough from an upper floor.

Sitting behind a plain, dark laminate desk in the corner of the lobby is Frau Weber, the building's Hausmeister. She is a woman in her early fifties, with a face that seems set in a permanent expression of neutral observation. Her mousy brown hair is cut short and tightly permed into a helmet-like style that was fashionable a decade ago and has now settled into a look of timeless, severe practicality.

She isn't wearing a uniform, but her clothes serve as one. Today, it's a mustard-yellow polyester blouse, buttoned all the way to the collar, with a heavy, machine-knitted brown cardigan worn over it despite the stuffy indoor heat. Her posture is perfectly straight, her hands resting on either side of a large, open ledger on her desk. Next to the ledger sits a black rotary phone and a single, neatly sharpened pencil.

Her eyes, small and alert behind large, sensible spectacles, are her most active feature. They don't miss anything. As the postal worker enters, her gaze lifts from her ledger and settles on him, not with curiosity, but with a flat, cataloging intensity. She observes the letters he sorts, her expression unchanging, her stillness a stark contrast to his routine movements. She is a piece of the lobby's furniture, as permanent and watchful as the building itself.

The moment Frau Weber saw the postal worker, a silent understanding passed between them. The lobby's placid music seemed to thin as they performed the state-required greeting.

"Wille." Her voice was low as she brought a fist to her chest, then extended her arm, palm flat and facing the floor.

"Wille," he echoed, the motion crisp and automatic. The younger receptionist at the adjacent desk remained oblivious, lost in her own world.

Relaxing, the postal worker approached the wall of mailboxes that dominated the lobby. With a masterkey, he opened the array, revealing hundreds of empty slots. He began his methodical work, his hands a blur of paper and metal. When he reached the letter for apartment 708, his rhythm broke. He held it for a beat longer than the others, his eyes double-checking the name against the label on the box before slotting it home. Locking the panel, he left as quietly as he had arrived.

At 10:30 AM, a woman with thick, voluminous wavy brown hair entered the lobby, a small, restless figure trailing at her side. It was Katharina Schröder, dressed for the cool October air in a long, camel-colored wool coat that fell below her knees. Beneath it, she wore a simple dark green turtleneck and high-waisted, charcoal-gray trousers. Her leather boots clicked softly on the beech-patterned laminate floor with each purposeful step as she led her son across the wide, empty space.

As they reached the lobby's center, she walked past the reception desk without a word, offering no acknowledgment to the Hausmeister, Frau Weber. The Hausmeister took note, her expression unchanging; it was the usual coldness she received from this woman. Katharina had always been like that. Her son, on the other hand, was not.

"Muttiiii, please don't check your mails again, I want to go outtt..." he whined, his voice echoing slightly in the quiet lobby.

"I'll be quick, Heimer," Katharina replied, her voice low. She paused at the wall of mailboxes, rubbing his head as she fished a keyring from her coat pocket, a simple, worn leather fob. Attached to the metal ring, alongside the key for apartment 708 and a miniature trowel. With a jangle of keys, she opened their box, number 708. She pulled out a few letters, and as she stacked them to examine the senders, her movements froze. She had come across the thick envelope with the official eagle seal of the MVK.

Her eyes widened almost imperceptibly. With a sharp crack, she broke the seal and unfolded the letter, her gaze scanning the dense, bureaucratic text.

"Mommmm, why are you taking so long?" Heimer complained again, tugging at her coat sleeve.

... As she read the letter thoroughly.

A warm, practiced smile touched her lips as she looked down at him. She reached out to pet his head playfully, and he ducked away with an annoyed squirm. "You'll be going to school soon, dear," she said, her voice steady despite the weight of the letter in her hand. "They've just sent the directive."

Heimer's face fell. "What? But I don't want to go to school now! My break has just started like a few months ago!"

Katharina crouched down to his level, forcing a bright, cheerful expression. "But aren't you excited to make new friends, dear?"

He crossed his little arms, unconvinced. "I don't want to. I had enough friends here."

She sighed, a quiet, weary sound. "I know. But it's time."

He pouted, breaking eye contact to stare at the polished floor, a silent, stubborn protest in the hopes she might relent. Kneeling down, she gently took his face in her hands, turning him back to face her.

"Come on, baby, look at me," she murmured, her voice soft. "It'll be much more fun than kindergarten. What if you make friends with a girl, and she likes you, hm?"

His face scrunched up in disgust. "What, no! Ew! I don't like girls..."

A genuine, fleeting chuckle escaped her lips. "And why is that?"

"They are... they're just too... I don't know." He couldn't find the words, only the feeling.

"Trust me, darling," she said, her smile not quite reaching her eyes. "It'll be thrilling, okay?" She leaned in and pecked his cheek before rising to her feet, the official letter a cold weight in her pocket.
